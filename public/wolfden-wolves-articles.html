The Minnesota Timberwolves are 20-6 at home, but they are 12-15 on the road.

Jimmy Butler: MVP Candidate

By: Devan Olsem

The Minnesota Timberwolves are currently sitting at fourth in the Western Conference with a record of 22-13, their best record at this point in the season in over a decade. The biggest reason the Wolves are where they are? Two words, one name: Jimmy Butler. The Wolves acquired Butler from the Bulls this offseason, and Minnesota fans have not been disappointed. Butler is averaging 21.2 points per game this season, 20th in the NBA. In addition, Butler is also averaging 5.5 rebounds and 4.8 assists per game. Yes, those numbers are impressive, but what is even more impressive is what Butler has done in the month of December. This month, Butler is averaging a clean 27 PPG, 5.5 RPG, and 5.2 APG. Butler started the season off slow in terms of scoring the basketball, but it seems like he has certainly found his groove in the month of December.

While his offensive firepower has certainly helped the Wolves this season, his defensive skills and his killer mentality have also played a big role in why the Wolves currently sit at 22-13. The Timberwolves have not had an “F you” type player since Kevin Garnett. Butler is just that and more. Butler cares about one thing and one thing only: winning, a satisfying sight for Wolves’ fans. Likewise, Butler is carrying the Wolves on the defensive end. The Timberwolves currently sit at 25th in the league in defensive rating (108.0). However, if not for Jimmy Butler and Taj Gibson, the Wolves would almost certainly be last in that category. It seems as if Butler and Gibson are the only players that always seem to be in the right position on defense. Yes, they are veterans in the NBA, and yes they have played in Thibodeau’s system before, but to give 100% effort on the defensive end night in and night out is impressive.

Timberwolves Early Season Player Grades

By: Devan Olsem

Karl-Anthony Towns: B

Entering his third NBA season, Karl-Anthony Towns was expected to continue his offensive power, but also improve on his lackluster defense. Towns has not disappointed on the offensive side of the ball, for the most part. Towns is averaging 20.9 points per game, while shooting 52.9% from the field and 35.6% from three-point range. Towns is coming off of a terrific showing Sunday against the Suns posting 32 points on 12-26 shooting. Towns finally got his touches and shots Sunday against Phoenix and it payed off in a big way. The reason Towns is graded just a B is because of his defense. Towns has had a hard time defending early in his NBA career and that has continued this season. Towns cannot seem to decide if he wants to come over and block a shot, or stay with his guy in the post, that leaves KAT not committing to either and leaving the team in a tough position. Towns needs to either commit to getting a block and going up completely on the shooter, or box out his man and prevent an offensive rebound. Obviously the season is only about 25% of the way over and there is still time for KAT to improve his defense and his B letter grade.

Andrew Wiggins: B+

Coming into the season, the big question would be when Andrew Wiggins would sign his max-level contract with Minnesota. It was never really in question, but the process took a bit longer than many people expected. Part of being offered a max contract with the Wolves was a face-to-face meeting with Timberwolves owner, Glen Taylor. Taylor wanted to meet with Wiggins and make sure he was committed to growing on both ends of the floor. And that is exactly what Wiggins has been doing so far this season. Wiggins is averaging 1.4 steals per game, which is a career-high. In addition to his steals, Wiggins looks much more invested on the defensive end of the court. Wiggins has been hustling all season and putting his body on the line chasing loose balls. Maybe, Jimmy Butler is starting to rub off on the young gun from Canada. Along with his improvements on defense, Wiggins has continued to put the ball in the basket. Although it was inevitable that his points per game would go down with Jimmy Butler now in town, Wiggins is still averaging 18.9 points per game on 45.5% shooting from the field. Wiggins has been very efficient on two-point field goals shooting a career-best 50.4% from that range. However, the three-point shot has not been kind to Wiggins so far this season. Wiggins is shooting just 31.7% from three-point distance this season while attempting the most threes per game in his career (4.1). To improve on his B+ grade, Wiggins needs to improve his three-point shot and become an even more complete player. Wiggins is still averaging just 1.8 assists per game, and with the offensive weapons the Wolves have, that number should be much higher.

Timberwolves: The good, bad, great, and ugly

By: Devan Olsem

The Good

Jeff Teague: In the offseason, the Minnesota Timberwolves decided to move on from longtime point guard, Ricky Rubio. After trading Rubio to Utah, they brought in a new face to run the show at the point guard position: Jeff Teague. Teague has been inconsistent so far this season, but all in all, he has been a key part of the Timberwolves early success. Teague holds the second best three-point percentage on the team knocking down 40% of his threes so far this season. In addition, Teague is posting a team-high 7.3 assists per night. However, Teague does have some areas to improve upon. First, he needs to be more consistent. Teague will have nights where he lacks aggression and it hurts the team. When Teague is being aggressive, and finding the open man, the team typically plays well. Second, he needs to improve his shooting in the paint. He is shooting just 44.8% in the paint this season, well below league average. One of the biggest knocks on former Timberwolf point guard Ricky Rubio was his inability to finish at the rim. Last season, Rubio shot 44.9% in the paint for the Wolves, so it is safe to say that Teague needs to improve on his percentage. Lastly, Teague needs to have better communication on defense and work with Karl-Anthony Towns to figure out how to defend pick-and-rolls. Opposing teams are shooting the ball at a very high clip against the Timberwolves, and one of the biggest reasons for that is their inability to guard pick-and-rolls. This poor pick-and-roll defense has recently been on display in losses against Detroit and Charlotte. While Teague does have areas to improve upon, all in all, he has been a bright spot for the team this season.

The Bad

Timberwolves’ three-point shooting: Coming into the season, most people were concerned about the Timberwolves’ ability to knock down shots from deep. 17 games into the season and those worries have not gone away. While the Timberwolves are shooting 35.8% from three-point range this season, 16th in the NBA, they are only making 8.2 threes a game, third worst in the NBA. The Timberwolves do not have a lot of three-point shooters on their roster, but the hope was that the Wolves could cancel that out by all of the other fire power they have on the scoring end with Jimmy Butler, Karl-Anthony Towns, and Andrew Wiggins. However, when the Timberwolves have gotten behind in games, it has been hard for them to crawl back into the contest since they are not making a lot of threes. Against big name opponents like the Golden State Warriors and the Houston Rockets, the Timberwolves will need to have players that have the ability to hit shots from deep.

Timberwolves: Playoffs or Bust?

By: Devan Olsem

When the Minnesota Timberwolves open up the 2017-18 season in San Antonio on October 18th, they will have a very different look. The Timberwolves will only have six players from last seasons roster return this year, unless Shabazz Muhammad decides to return on a veterans minimum deal before the start of the season. Although the Wolves will only be returning six players from last years roster, it may not be the worst case scenario. Minnesota has not made the playoffs since the 2003-04 season, and they were just 31-51 last season, 13th in the Western Conference.

The young Wolves team was projected to make a big jump last season when new coach Tom Thibodeau was hired. However, that was not the case. The Wolves struggled early, especially on the defensive side of the ball. They started playing well in the middle of the season trying to make a late playoff push, but in the end, it was all for naught. Now, the Minnesota Timberwolves are coming off one of their most important offseasons in recent history that saw the team make a load of changes.

The first change, and biggest, came on the night of the 2017 NBA Draft. Leading up to the draft, there was some buzz that the Timberwolves could look to move out of the seventh overall pick, but nobody expected a blockbuster deal to happen. Just moments before the draft opened, it was announced that the Timberwolves had shipped out the seventh pick, Zach LaVine, and Kris Dunn to the Chicago Bulls for Jimmy Butler and the 16th overall selection in the draft that night, which they used to select Justin Patton. NBA fans around the world were in awe. Wolves fans, accustomed to progressing players and trading away stars, were mostly excited that they were finally on the receiving end of a star being traded. Butler, one of the top two-way players in the league, played for Tom Thibodeau for four seasons before the Bulls let Thibs go. Butler will make an immediate impact especially on the defensive end of the court, where the Wolves struggled mightily last season.
